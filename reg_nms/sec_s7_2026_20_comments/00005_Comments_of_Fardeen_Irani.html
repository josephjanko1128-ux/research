Subject: File No. S7-2026-20
From: Fardeen Irani

Dear Secretary, I am submitting this comment letter in response to the Securities and Exchange Commission’s ("Commission") proposal to amend Regulation NMS under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 by rescinding Rule 611 (the trade-through prohibition), Rule 610(e) (restrictions on locking and crossing quotations), and the related defined terms in Rule 600. While the elimination of these rules represents a significant shift toward a disclosure-based, free-market routing architecture, the Commission must carefully evaluate how the removal of trade-through protections interacts with the systemic internalization of retail order flow. The empirical data cited in related market structure reviews underscores severe inefficiencies in the current retail ecosystem that must be addressed concurrently through a heightened, robust enforcement of the broker’s fiduciary duty of Best Execution. Key Data The Commission Must Weigh *1. High Rates of Internalization at Sub-Optimal Prices* Commission data demonstrates that depending on the specific NMS stock type, its price, and its liquidity, between 46% and 73% of retail marketable order shares are currently internalized by wholesalers at a price worse than the NBBO midpoint. Wholesalers currently provide customers with executions at the midpoint or better for only about 46% of customer orders executed in a principal capacity. *2. Unused, Accessible Midpoint Liquidity on Exchanges and ATSs* Most critically, the data shows that between 40% and 93% of the shares in marketable retail orders that wholesalers internalize at prices less favorable than the NBBO midpoint actually had midpoint liquidity available at a better price on a public exchange or an Alternative Trading System (ATS). For stocks priced higher than $30, between 60% and 93% of these principal-capacity wholesaler executions had unexecuted, superior midpoint liquidity sitting readily available on transparent venues. *3. Impact on Midpoint Pricing Dynamics and Spread Calculations* Under the newly adopted modernizations of Rules 605 and 612, the definition of the NBBO midpoint becomes highly dynamic. For example, if the National Best Bid (NBB) is $260.00 and the National Best Offer (NBO) is $260.10, the midpoint is $260.05. A sub-penny or small odd-lot buy quotation (e.g., 40 shares at $260.02) changes the NBBO midpoint to $260.06. This mathematical shift lowers the calculated effective spread for subsequent buy orders but raises it for sell orders. If the Commission rescinds Rule 611 and 610(e), markets will naturally lock and cross. In a locked market, the spread collapses to 0%, making the execution price and the NBBO midpoint identical. In crossed markets, temporary negative spreads will exist. Without rigid federal routing protocols, retail brokers will have to aggressively adapt their Smart Order Routers (SORs) to navigate these shifting midpoints accurately. *4. Accurately Disclosing "Price Impact" and Adverse Selection* As the Commission transitions away from algorithmic micro-management, public accountability through Rule 605 reporting becomes the primary safeguard for investors. "Price impact"—the extent to which the NBBO midpoint moves against a liquidity provider—serves as a vital metric for measuring adverse selection costs. While Rule 605 historical disclosures rely on a five-minute post-execution window, utilizing the modernized one-minute post-execution window (as seen in recent Consolidated Audit Trail analyses) is necessary to accurately reflect modern trading speeds. Conclusion & Recommendation Rescinding Rules 611 and 610(e) will successfully strip away decades of technical complexity, remove artificial barriers between "protected round lots" and "unprotected odd lots," and eliminate the operational burdens of preventing locked or crossed markets. However, because up to 93% of internalized retail orders are bypassed despite superior midpoint liquidity sitting on exchanges, the SEC cannot rely on deregulation alone. If the trade-through rule is eliminated, the Commission must strictly mandate that retail brokers actively scan all public and private venues to capture these available midpoint prices. The fiduciary mandate of Best Execution must be fiercely enforced to ensure that retail investors are not continually internalized at inferior prices when superior liquidity is openly displayed on the tape. Regulatory & Fiduciary Enhancements *1. Aggressive Enforcement of FINRA Rule 5310 (Best Execution)* Without Rule 611 forcing a trade to route to the best price, regulators must aggressively penalize brokers who blindly route to a captive wholesaler. Under an enhanced Best Execution mandate, brokers must prove they regularly sample and route to venues that display superior midpoint liquidity. *2. Mandating "Midpoint Cross" Priority* Regulators could mandate that before a wholesaler is legally permitted to internalize an order in a principal capacity, they must systematically ping public exchanges or Alternative Trading Systems (ATS) to ensure no passive midpoint liquidity is being bypassed. *3. Standardizing the One-Minute Rule 605 Metric* To prevent brokers from hiding poor executions, the SEC should permanently shorten the Rule 605 "Price Impact" window from five minutes to the modern one-minute standard. This forces wholesalers to publicly disclose accurate, near-instantaneous adverse selection data, making it easier for retail brokers to fire underperforming wholesalers. Technological & Algorithmic Solutions *1. Dynamic Smart Order Routers (SORs)* Retail brokerages must upgrade their internal routing algorithms. Instead of sending 100% of marketable retail orders directly to a single wholesaler via a Payment for Order Flow (PFOF) agreement, SORs must dynamically split orders, routing portions directly to exchanges (like IEX, Nasdaq, or NYSE) when those venues display active odd-lot or round-lot midpoint liquidity. *2. Midpoint-Pegged Retail Orders* Exchanges can introduce specific order types tailored for retail investors that automatically "peg" to the shifting NBBO midpoint. As seen in the scenario analyzed above, if a 40-share quote shifts the midpoint from $260.05 to $260.06, automated exchange systems should instantly re-price the retail order to capture that micro-penny optimization without human intervention. *3. Intermarket Sweep Orders (ISOs) for Retail* Brokers can utilize specialized routing tech to clear out superior displayed retail odd lots across multiple fragmented venues simultaneously, rather than allowing a wholesaler to internalize the block at an inferior wholesale price. Market Structure & Structural Reforms *1. Harmonization of Variable Tick Sizes (Rule 612)* Public exchanges historically lost order flow because they were legally blocked from quoting in sub-pennies, while off-exchange wholesalers could trade at any fraction of a cent. Fully implementing Rule 612's variable sub-penny tick sizes allows public exchanges to compete directly with wholesalers, narrowing the spread and naturally drawing retail order flow back to transparent books. *2. Reforming Payment for Order Flow (PFOF)* The economic incentive driving sub-optimal internalization is PFOF—wholesalers paying brokers for exclusive access to "uninformed" retail trades. Phasing out or heavily restricting PFOF would force retail brokerages to compete strictly on execution quality (price improvement) rather than maximizing secondary routing revenue. *3. The Inverted-Spread Arbitrage Mechanism* Once Rule 610(e) is rescinded, markets will experience brief "crossed" periods where the bid is higher than the ask. High-frequency trading (HFT) firms will naturally rush to arbitrage these inverted spreads. This algorithmic competition will naturally collapse inefficient spreads, automatically pushing prices back to a highly efficient midpoint without needing a regulatory mandate. Thank you for considering these comments. Sincerely, Fardeen Irani
