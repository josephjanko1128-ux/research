To: The Office of the Secretary, Securities and Exchange Commission From: Mary Smith Date: June 11, 2026 Re: Opposition to the Proposed Rescission of Rule 611 and Rule 610(e) of Regulation NMS Dear Secretary, I am writing to express my strong opposition to the Commission’s proposal to rescind Rule 611 (the "Order Protection Rule") and Rule 610(e) of Regulation NMS. As an individual retail investor, I believe these rules provide essential safeguards that ensure I receive fair execution quality for my trades. Removing them, without a robust and proven replacement, risks compromising the integrity of the retail investor experience. 1. The "Best Price" Backstop For years, the Trade-Through Rule has served as a critical backstop for the duty of "best execution." It provides a clear, objective standard, the National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO), that I can trust when I place an order. If these rules are rescinded, I am concerned that the responsibility for ensuring I receive the best possible price will fall entirely onto my broker-dealer’s internal routing practices. Without the objective "backstop" of Rule 611, there is a significant risk that retail orders could be executed at inferior prices, essentially subsidizing institutional latency advantages at the expense of everyday participants. 2. Erosion of Market Transparency The current regulatory framework, by prohibiting locked and crossed markets, promotes price discovery and prevents the confusion caused by contradictory quotes. If Rule 610(e) is removed, I fear the market will become increasingly fragmented and opaque. For a retail investor, the simplicity of the NBBO is a primary indicator of market health. If the market becomes a collection of disparate "dark" and "lit" pools with varying price protections, the ability for an individual to confidently enter the market will diminish. 3. The Risk to Retail Trust Retail participation in the markets is at an all-time high, driven by the belief that U.S. markets are the fairest and most transparent in the world. Repealing these foundational rules signals that the Commission is prioritizing the efficiency of high-frequency trading and institutional routing over the price-protection of the individual. If retail investors feel that the "playing field" is being tilted toward professional traders who can navigate a more complex, unregulated landscape, we may see a decline in public trust and participation. Conclusion While I understand the arguments that these rules may impose compliance costs or contribute to market fragmentation, these are costs of maintaining a fair system. I urge the Commission to reject the proposal to rescind these rules. If the Commission believes the current rules are outdated, it should focus on modernizing them to reflect current market realities, such as improving the definition of protected quotes or adjusting access fees, rather than removing the protections that guarantee a baseline of fairness for all investors. Thank you for your time and for the opportunity to comment on this vital issue. Sincerely, Mary Smith
