Subject: File No. S7-2026-20
From: Joe Kirchner

To the Securities and Exchange Commission: I write to oppose the proposed rescission of Rule 611 (the Order Protection Rule) and Rule 610(e) (the prohibition on locking and crossing quotations) of Regulation NMS. While I share the Commission's interest in modernizing market structure, I believe the rapid integration of artificial intelligence into trading and order routing makes these protections more necessary, not less. The proposal rests on the premise that best-execution obligations can substitute for the bright-line guarantee that Rule 611 provides. But best execution is a subjective, principles-based standard, and in an environment where routing, pricing, and liquidity decisions are increasingly made by AI and machine-learning systems operating at microsecond speed, subjective standards are far harder to monitor and enforce. The Order Protection Rule offers an objective, machine-verifiable benchmark — a displayed price that either was or was not honored. Removing it would shift oversight from a clear rule to the inspection of opaque, adaptive algorithms whose decision logic regulators, brokers, and investors cannot readily reconstruct or audit. This widens, rather than closes, the accountability gap. AI-driven trading also sharpens the asymmetry between sophisticated and ordinary investors. Firms deploying advanced models to predict order flow and optimize execution stand to benefit most from the removal of a baseline price guarantee, while retail and less-sophisticated investors — who cannot monitor execution quality across dozens of venues in real time, let alone audit the algorithms routing their orders — bear the downside. A bright-line protection like Rule 611 is precisely the kind of safeguard that does not depend on an investor's technological resources to be effective. I am further concerned about the interaction between AI systems and displayed liquidity. The Order Protection Rule rewards participants who post the best displayed prices by ensuring those quotes are honored. In a market where AI agents continuously adjust quoting strategies, removing that assurance could accelerate the migration of liquidity into opaque venues and incentivize models to deprioritize public quote display, degrading price discovery for everyone. Permitting locked and crossed markets under the rescission of Rule 610(e) compounds this risk: AI systems optimizing within an environment of locked and crossed quotations may produce emergent, hard-to-predict behavior and feedback loops that the Commission has not analyzed. Finally, the Commission has not demonstrated that the claimed compliance-cost savings would offset these risks, nor has it studied how autonomous and increasingly adaptive trading systems would behave once these guardrails are removed. Empirical analysis from a pre-AI market structure cannot be assumed to hold in markets now shaped by machine intelligence. Before dismantling protections that have underpinned U.S. equity markets for nearly two decades, the Commission should conduct robust analysis specifically addressing AI-driven trading dynamics, and should consider narrower, targeted reforms rather than wholesale rescission. For these reasons, I respectfully urge the Commission to withdraw the proposed amendments. Respectfully submitted, Joe Kirchner
