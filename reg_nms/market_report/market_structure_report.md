# SEC Rulemaking Comment Letter Analysis: Rule 611 (Order Protection Rule) and Rule 610(e) Rescission Proposal
### File No. S7-2026-20 | Regulation NMS Reform

---

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes the full comment letter record submitted in response to the SEC's proposal to rescind Rule 611 (the Order Protection Rule / Trade-Through Rule) and Rule 610(e) (the prohibition on locked and crossed markets) under Regulation NMS. The comment record spans 30 discrete submissions from a diverse range of stakeholders, including academic researchers, registered broker-dealers, individual retail investors, an SEC internal memorandum, and several non-substantive or off-topic filings.

**The dominant sentiment in the substantive comment record is opposition to outright rescission**, driven overwhelmingly by retail investor commenters who view Rule 611 as an essential, hard mechanical safeguard against inferior order execution. However, the most analytically sophisticated submissions — from academic economists, a specialized broker-dealer, and technically literate individual commenters — present a more nuanced picture: they acknowledge structural deficiencies in the current framework while warning that rescission without a carefully designed replacement regime risks creating a regulatory vacuum that could harm retail investors and degrade market quality.

Several critical themes emerge across the record:

1. **Best execution as a substitute for Rule 611 is widely viewed as insufficient**, particularly for retail investors who cannot monitor routing decisions or audit algorithmic execution logic.
2. **Payment for Order Flow (PFOF) and wholesaler internalization** are identified as the root structural problems that Rule 611 has failed to solve, and which rescission alone will not address.
3. **The QCT exemption gap** represents a specific, technically urgent concern raised by a specialized broker-dealer that the Commission appears not to have addressed in the proposal.
4. **Tokenized equities and blockchain-based trading venues** are identified as a forward-looking rationale for rescission, but also as a source of new regulatory complexity requiring careful treatment.
5. **The empirical market structure data** provided by the SEC's own Division of Trading and Markets confirms dramatic growth in off-exchange trading — over 50% of NASDAQ volume now executes off-exchange — lending credibility to both sides of the debate.

The Commission faces a genuine policy dilemma: the current framework has demonstrably failed to incentivize displayed liquidity, yet the proposed remedy of outright rescission without a robust replacement framework risks accelerating the very trends it seeks to address.

## Major Themes

### Theme 1: The Adequacy of Best Execution as a Substitute for Rule 611

This is the single most contested issue in the record. **Every substantive commenter who opposes rescission cites the inadequacy of best execution as a replacement mechanism.** The arguments range from the sophisticated (Malinova & Park's citation of European empirical data showing 14-21% of orders fail to execute at the best available price under a "reasonable steps" standard) to the intuitive (retail investors noting they cannot audit routing algorithms or monitor execution quality across dozens of venues).

Even commenters who conditionally support rescission (Malinova & Park, Fardeen Irani) insist that best execution must be dramatically strengthened — not merely preserved — to serve as the primary investor protection mechanism. The European cautionary tale is cited repeatedly: a principles-based standard without a clear benchmark produces measurable, systematic execution quality shortfalls.

Jeff Martinez frames this most sharply: best execution is "a principles-based, facts-and-circumstances standard enforced after the fact. It instructs a broker to seek favorable terms. It does not tie the prices of competing venues together, and it is not a structural price-discovery mechanism."

### Theme 2: Payment for Order Flow and Wholesaler Internalization as the Root Problem

Multiple commenters — spanning retail investors, the RIA/former floor broker, and the technically sophisticated individual commenter — identify PFOF and wholesaler internalization as the structural disease that Rule 611 has failed to cure, and which rescission will not address. Fardeen Irani's empirical data is particularly striking: between 40% and 93% of internalized retail orders that received sub-midpoint prices had superior midpoint liquidity available on public exchanges or ATSs at the time of execution. This suggests the problem is not a lack of available liquidity but a misalignment of routing incentives.

Jared Albert, writing as a retail investor who actively posts limit orders on lit exchanges, provides a ground-level perspective: his resting orders are routinely traded through by FINRA-reported off-exchange executions, which he characterizes as a structural failure of best execution enforcement rather than a market structure design problem.

### Theme 3: The Incentive to Display and the Free-Rider Problem

A recurring structural concern across the most sophisticated submissions is the free-rider problem: dark orders execute at prices formed by displayed quotes without contributing to price formation. Jeff Martinez articulates this most clearly: "A hidden or dark order can execute at the national best bid or offer, a price formed by some other participant who posted a displayed quote and bore the information leakage and adverse-selection risk of showing their hand."

Malinova and Park raise the complementary concern that rescinding Rule 611 removes the priority protection currently afforded to displayed limit orders, potentially weakening the incentive to post aggressive displayed quotes. The SEC's own data confirms the trend: dark trading now exceeds 50% of NASDAQ volume, up from 29.4% in 2005.

### Theme 4: Tokenized Equities and Blockchain Compatibility

Malinova and Park raise a forward-looking structural argument that receives no substantive rebuttal in the record: Rule 611 is physically incompatible with blockchain-based trading systems. On-chain venues execute trades at discrete block intervals via smart contract calls; real-time cross-system routing is impossible. Automated market makers price continuously based on pool state, creating an irresolvable compliance paradox if the OPR were extended to tokenized equity venues.

Jeff Martinez takes the opposite view on the policy implication: he argues that if a token conveys the ownership and economic interest of a publicly traded NMS equity, it should trade under the same displayed-priority discipline regardless of its technological wrapper. The regulatory category should follow the underlying asset, not the token.

### Theme 5: The QCT Exemption Gap

FOG Equities raises a specific, technically urgent concern that appears to have been overlooked in the Commission's proposal: rescinding Rule 611 eliminates the Qualified Contingent Trade (QCT) exemption without providing a replacement safe harbor. The QCT exemption allows the stock leg of a complex options order to be priced outside the NBBO when the economics of the contingent transaction are predetermined. Without a replacement, each such equity hedge becomes subject to after-the-fact best execution scrutiny under FINRA Rule 5310, creating legal uncertainty that will cause options market makers to widen spreads, reduce quoted size, or decline to quote certain complex series altogether.

This concern is notable because it identifies a specific, concrete harm to a specific market segment — the complex listed options market — that is entirely distinct from the retail investor protection debate dominating the rest of the record.

### Theme 6: AI and Algorithmic Trading Complexity

Joe Kirchner raises a distinctive concern not addressed by other commenters: the rapid integration of AI and machine-learning systems into trading makes bright-line protections like Rule 611 more necessary, not less. His argument is that best execution is a subjective standard that is "far harder to monitor and enforce" when routing and pricing decisions are made by opaque, adaptive algorithms operating at microsecond speed. Rule 611 provides "an objective, machine-verifiable benchmark — a displayed price that either was or was not honored." Removing it shifts oversight to the inspection of algorithms whose decision logic regulators, brokers, and investors cannot readily reconstruct.

## Economic Arguments

### Arguments Supporting Rescission or Reform

**1. The OPR's routing mandate creates exploitable latency arbitrage (Malinova & Park)**
The most rigorous economic argument in the record. The OPR's mandatory cross-venue order splitting creates predictable routing patterns that fast traders exploit through latency arbitrage. The arbitrage is profitable as a direct consequence of regulatory compliance requirements, not informational advantages. Removing the mandate removes the primary source of this cross-market predictability.

**2. Rule 611 has failed to incentivize displayed liquidity over two decades (Martinez, SEC T&M Memo)**
Off-exchange trading regularly exceeds 50% of total volume. The maker-taker rebate of approximately $0.0020 per share is insufficient compensation for the adverse-selection and information-leakage costs of posting a visible quote. The scheduled reduction of the access fee cap from $0.0030 to $0.0010 per share will further narrow the economic margin for displayed liquidity.

**3. Dark orders free-ride on prices formed by displayed quotes (Martinez)**
Non-displayed orders execute at prices formed by displayed quotes without contributing to price formation, creating a structural subsidy for dark trading at the expense of displayed liquidity providers. This is a market failure that Rule 611 has not corrected.

**4. PFOF creates a misaligned incentive structure (Irani, Martinez, Albert)**
Wholesalers pay brokers for exclusive access to uninformed retail order flow. Between 40% and 93% of internalized retail orders that received sub-midpoint prices had superior midpoint liquidity available on public venues at the time of execution. The problem is routing incentives, not liquidity availability.

**5. Blockchain incompatibility creates irresolvable compliance burdens (Malinova & Park)**
On-chain venues execute trades at discrete block intervals; real-time cross-system routing is physically impossible. Automated market makers price continuously based on pool state, creating a compliance paradox if the OPR were extended to tokenized equity venues.

### Arguments Against Rescission

**1. Rescission removes the only cross-venue price-discovery discipline (Martinez)**
Best execution is a principles-based, after-the-fact enforcement standard. It does not tie the prices of competing venues together and is not a structural price-discovery mechanism. Removing Rule 611 without replacement removes the only mechanism that kept executions tethered to the best displayed quote.

**2. European best execution experience demonstrates measurable shortfalls (Malinova & Park)**
Under the European "all reasonable steps" standard, only 79-86% of marketable orders in Eurostoxx50 stocks execute at the best available price. A 14-21% execution quality shortfall is a concrete, empirically documented harm that a weak best execution standard produces.

**3. Rescission will accelerate dark trading, not reverse it (Martinez)**
The underlying incentive failure — insufficient reward for displaying quotes — remains equally broken after rescission. Removing the rule treats the complexity while abandoning the discipline, and is likely to accelerate the flight to dark trading rather than reverse it.

**4. The QCT exemption gap creates concrete harm to options market liquidity (McDevitt)**
The Commission's own 2006 findings in Release No. 34-54389 — that breaking up contingent trade components could leave parties out of hedge, prevent dealers from committing capital, and disadvantage the market — remain equally valid today. Rescinding Rule 611 without a replacement safe harbor reintroduces precisely the harm the QCT exemption was designed to prevent.

**5. AI-driven markets require objective, machine-verifiable benchmarks (Kirchner)**
In an environment where routing and pricing decisions are made by opaque, adaptive algorithms at microsecond speed, subjective standards are far harder to monitor and enforce. Rule 611 provides an objective benchmark that does not depend on an investor's technological resources to be effective.

**6. Compliance cost savings are undemonstrated (Kirchner, Ravnitzky)**
The Commission has not demonstrated that compliance-cost savings from rescission would offset the risks created by removing these protections. Empirical analysis derived from a pre-AI market structure cannot be assumed to hold in markets now shaped by machine intelligence.

## Liquidity Arguments

### Pro-Rescission Liquidity Arguments

**1. OPR routing mandate exposes liquidity-seeking orders to sniping (Malinova & Park)**
Cross-market order splitting mandated by the OPR, while intended to ensure best prices, paradoxically exposes liquidity-seeking orders to sniping by fast traders who anticipate subsequent legs of split orders arriving at other venues.

**2. Locked markets collapse spreads to zero, benefiting retail investors (Irani)**
Post-Rule 610(e) rescission, locked markets (zero spread) make execution price and NBBO midpoint identical. Crossed markets will create temporary negative spreads that HFT arbitrage will naturally resolve, restoring efficient midpoint pricing without regulatory intervention.

**3. Variable tick sizes under Rule 612 would draw order flow back to lit venues (Irani)**
Exchanges historically lost retail order flow because they were legally prohibited from quoting in sub-pennies while wholesalers faced no such restriction. Full Rule 612 implementation restores competitive parity, potentially drawing retail order flow back to transparent lit books.

### Anti-Rescission Liquidity Arguments

**1. Rescission removes priority protection for displayed limit orders (Malinova & Park, Martinez)**
Rule 611 currently protects investors who post the best displayed prices by giving their orders priority over worse-priced orders. Rescission removes this protection and may weaken the incentive to display aggressive limit orders, with broader negative implications for price discovery.

**2. Superior midpoint liquidity is available but systematically bypassed (Irani, Albert)**
Between 40% and 93% of internalized retail orders that received sub-midpoint prices had superior midpoint liquidity available on public exchanges or ATSs at the time of execution. The liquidity problem is one of access and incentives, not supply.

**3. Rescission will accelerate migration to dark venues (Martinez, Kirchner)**
Without the incentive to display, AI quoting strategies will deprioritize public quote display, accelerating the migration of liquidity into opaque venues and degrading price discovery for all market participants.

**4. Options market liquidity depends on QCT safe harbor certainty (McDevitt)**
The stock leg of a complex options order is the hedge that allows options market makers to quote multi-leg strategies confidently and in size. Without a defined safe harbor, each equity hedge priced outside the NBBO becomes subject to after-the-fact best execution scrutiny, making the hedge legally uncertain and operationally unreliable.

**5. Declining average trade size alongside rising volume indicates dispersed, harder-to-aggregate liquidity (SEC T&M Memo)**
Average trade size in NYSE-listed stocks fell from 777 shares in 2005 to 110 shares in 2026 (-86%), while total volume increased 50%. This suggests liquidity is more dispersed and harder to aggregate, with implications for institutional execution quality.

## Competition Arguments

### Pro-Rescission Competition Arguments

**1. OPR integration creates exploitable routing patterns that benefit fast traders (Malinova & Park)**
The OPR electronically integrates geographically dispersed market centers, but this integration comes at the cost of creating exploitable routing patterns that benefit fast traders at the expense of other market participants.

**2. Blockchain venues cannot comply with OPR routing requirements (Malinova & Park)**
The inability of decentralized on-chain venues to comply with OPR routing requirements creates an unlevel competitive playing field that would either exclude blockchain venues from the NMS or impose irresolvable compliance burdens on traditional brokers.

**3. Rescission allows best execution to govern competition between traditional and emerging venues (Malinova & Park)**
Removing the hard routing mandate allows more organic market evolution, enabling best execution obligations rather than prescriptive rules to govern the interaction between traditional NMS venues and emerging decentralized trading platforms.

### Anti-Rescission Competition Arguments

**1. Broker-dealer routing conflicts distort competition (Martinez)**
The market force that failed — the incentive to display — is left exactly as broken after rescission as before. Broker-dealers route for their own economics rather than the customer's result, and rescission does not address this structural agency problem.

**2. PFOF concentrates retail order flow away from competitive lit markets (Martinez, Irani, Albert)**
Payment for order flow arrangements cause marketable retail orders to be routed to a small number of off-exchange wholesalers rather than to displayed venues, concentrating order flow away from competitive lit markets. Internalization of retail order flow by a small number of wholesalers (Citadel Securities, Virtu, Jane Street) drains the lit book of the very order flow that would tighten displayed spreads.

**3. Two-tier market structure eliminates genuine competition (Albert)**
The current structure creates two separate equity markets: a dark market where actual trading occurs and a lit market that serves only as a reference pricing mechanism. This is "capitalism without competition."

**4. AI-driven trading sharpens asymmetry between sophisticated firms and retail investors (Kirchner)**
Firms deploying advanced models to predict order flow and optimize execution stand to benefit most from removal of a baseline price guarantee. Retail investors cannot monitor execution quality across dozens of venues in real time or audit routing algorithms.

**5. Tokenization platforms are the primary beneficiaries of rescission (Martinez)**
Tokenization platforms (Securitize, Coinbase, Galaxy Digital) are identified as the most direct beneficiaries of rescission, raising the concern that the proposal may be shaped by the interests of new entrants rather than the investing public.

## Retail Investor Arguments

### The Core Retail Investor Concern

The dominant theme across retail investor submissions is a straightforward and consistent argument: **retail investors do not control order routing, venue selection, wholesaler arrangements, or execution algorithms, and therefore depend on regulatory protections.** Rule 611 provides a hard, mechanical safeguard that does not require retail investors to understand market microstructure, monitor execution quality across dozens of venues, or audit routing algorithms. Removing it without a proven replacement leaves retail investors exposed to harms they have no practical means to detect or challenge.

### Specific Retail Investor Arguments by Commenter

**Joe Lee:** Retail investors depend on brokers, best execution obligations, and clear market-wide price protection rules. Removing Rule 611 could make execution quality harder to evaluate, reduce the usefulness of the NBBO as a benchmark, and increase the risk that different brokers provide materially different fills for similar orders.

**Mary Smith:** Without the objective backstop of Rule 611, retail orders could be executed at inferior prices, effectively subsidizing institutional latency advantages at the expense of everyday participants. Retail participation in U.S. markets is at an all-time high due to belief in market fairness; rescission threatens this confidence.

**Bhavith S:** Zero-commission brokerages have inherent conflicts of interest because they generate revenue through PFOF rather than commissions. Without Rule 611, these brokerages will be financially incentivized to route orders to venues that pay the highest PFOF rather than venues offering the best price. The cryptocurrency market serves as a cautionary analogy: unregulated markets with no trade-through protection result in chronic slippage for everyday traders.

**Jared Albert:** Retail investors who place resting limit orders on lit exchanges are harmed when those orders are traded through by off-exchange executions at inferior prices. PFOF is characterized as a criminal kickback scheme that defrauds individual investors by ensuring orders never reach lit markets where better prices may exist. Both sides of a trade are harmed by off-exchange execution that bypasses available lit market liquidity.

**Joe Kirchner:** A bright-line protection like Rule 611 is precisely the kind of safeguard that does not depend on an investor's technological resources to be effective. Retail investors cannot monitor execution quality across multiple venues in real time, nor audit the algorithms routing their orders. Shifting from a bright-line rule to a principles-based best-execution standard leaves retail investors exposed to harms they have no practical means to detect or challenge.

**Fardeen Irani (Mixed):** Retail investors are systematically harmed when up to 93% of their orders are internalized despite superior prices being openly displayed on public venues. However, the solution is not to retain Rule 611 but to aggressively enforce best execution and mandate pre-internalization venue scanning.

**Jeff Martinez (Mixed):** The displayed spread is wide enough to improve upon precisely because two decades of internalization have drained the lit book of the very order flow that would tighten it. Wholesalers provide small per-order price improvements measured against a displayed spread that internalization has artificially widened; the apparent benefit to retail investors is illusory relative to what a deeper lit market would provide.

**Malinova & Park (Conditional Support):** Retail investors are identified as the most vulnerable population requiring continued protection following OPR rescission. A strong best-execution obligation anchored to a clear, measurable benchmark is recommended as the primary mechanism to protect retail investors in the absence of the OPR.

## Consensus Positions

Despite the sharp divide between supporters and opponents of rescission, several positions command broad consensus across the substantive comment record:

### 1. Best Execution Must Be Strengthened, Not Merely Preserved
**Consensus across:** Malinova & Park, Martinez, Irani, Ravnitzky, Lee, Smith, Bhavith S, Kirchner

Every substantive commenter who addresses the best execution question agrees that the current principles-based standard is insufficient to serve as the primary investor protection mechanism in the absence of Rule 611. The disagreement is about whether strengthened best execution can adequately substitute for Rule 611 (Malinova & Park, Irani) or whether it is structurally incapable of doing so regardless of how it is strengthened (Martinez, Kirchner, retail investors).

### 2. PFOF and Wholesaler Internalization Are Structural Problems That Must Be Addressed
**Consensus across:** Martinez, Irani, Albert, Bhavith S, Lee (implicitly)

The current internalization regime, driven by PFOF economics, systematically routes retail order flow away from lit markets and toward wholesalers who may not provide the best available prices. This is identified as a root cause of the displayed liquidity problem that Rule 611 has failed to solve.

### 3. Retail Investors Are the Most Vulnerable Population and Require Specific Protections
**Consensus across:** All substantive commenters

Every substantive commenter — including those who support rescission — identifies retail investors as the population most at risk from any weakening of market structure protections. The disagreement is about the mechanism of protection, not the need for it.

### 4. The Commission Should Monitor Post-Rescission Market Quality
**Consensus across:** Malinova & Park, Ravnitzky, Martinez (implicitly)

If rescission proceeds, the Commission should establish clear monitoring frameworks, specific Rule 605 metrics, and defined thresholds that would trigger regulatory intervention. The Commission should not assume that market forces will self-correct without oversight.

### 5. The QCT Exemption Gap Must Be Addressed Before or Concurrent with Finalization
**Raised by:** FOG Equities (McDevitt); not contradicted by any other commenter

No commenter disputes FOG Equities' characterization of the QCT gap as a material regulatory vacuum. The Commission's silence on this issue in the proposal is itself a concern.

### 6. Rule 605 Reporting Should Be Modernized
**Consensus across:** Irani, Ravnitzky

The current five-minute price impact measurement window is inadequate for modern trading speeds. Shortening it to one minute would improve the accuracy of adverse selection disclosure and enable retail brokers to identify and terminate relationships with underperforming wholesalers.

## Outlier Positions

### 1. Outright Support for Rescission Without Conditions (No Commenter)
Notably, **no substantive commenter unconditionally supports outright rescission** of Rule 611. Even Malinova and Park, the only commenters who explicitly support rescission, condition their support on the adoption of a robust, benchmark-anchored best execution obligation and ongoing monitoring of displayed liquidity.

### 2. Consolidated Lit Book with Strict Displayed-Order Priority (Martinez)
Jeff Martinez proposes a specific structural alternative — a consolidated lit book with strict displayed-order priority — that goes significantly beyond the Commission's proposal and beyond what any other commenter advocates. His proposal would codify a rule that non-displayed orders may never trade at parity with or ahead of a displayed order at the same price, effectively rebuilding the trade-through framework around a different incentive structure. This position is analytically sophisticated but finds no support or echo in the rest of the record.

### 3. Ban All Unexposed Off-Exchange Trading (Albert)
Jared Albert calls for banning all unexposed off-exchange trading, joining the regulatory approach of G7 and OECD peer nations. This is the most aggressive position in the record and finds no support from any other commenter, though it is consistent with the direction of international regulatory trends.

### 4. AI-Specific Empirical Analysis Required Before Any Reform (Kirchner)
Joe Kirchner's argument that the Commission must conduct robust empirical analysis specifically addressing AI-driven trading dynamics before dismantling existing protections is unique in the record. No other commenter raises the AI dimension as a threshold condition for reform.

### 5. Technology-Neutral Displayed-Priority Rule for Tokenized NMS Equities (Martinez)
Martinez's argument that the regulatory category should follow the underlying asset — not the token — such that tokenized NMS equities should trade under the same displayed-priority discipline as traditional equities regardless of their trading venue, is a distinctive and technically sophisticated position not echoed by other commenters.

### 6. Pre-Internalization Midpoint-Cross Check Mandate (Irani)
Fardeen Irani's specific proposal for a mandatory "Midpoint Cross" priority requirement — obligating wholesalers to systematically ping public exchanges and ATSs to confirm no superior passive midpoint liquidity is being bypassed before internalizing any retail order — is a technically novel proposal not raised by any other commenter.

## Recommended SEC Actions

The following recommended actions are synthesized from the substantive comment record, weighted by the analytical rigor and specificity of the underlying submissions:

### Immediate Priority Actions

**1. Address the QCT Exemption Gap Before or Concurrent with Finalization**
Issue a no-action letter or adopt a new standalone safe harbor providing bright-line certainty for the stock leg of complex listed options transactions, using conditions substantially equivalent to the seven elements of a qualified contingent trade set forth in Release No. 34-54389. This is the most time-sensitive and technically specific recommendation in the record, and the Commission's silence on it in the proposal is a material deficiency.
*Source: FOG Equities (McDevitt)*

**2. Establish a Robust, Benchmark-Anchored Best Execution Obligation**
If rescission proceeds, the Commission must establish a best execution obligation anchored to a specific, measurable benchmark — not a vague "all reasonable steps" standard. The European experience demonstrates that weak best execution standards produce measurable, systematic execution quality shortfalls. The benchmark should be enforceable, auditable, and specifically protective of retail investors.
*Source: Malinova & Park, Martinez, Irani, Ravnitzky*

**3. Publish Scenario Analyses and Monitoring Frameworks**
Before finalizing the proposal, publish scenario analyses showing the effects of different broker-dealer disconnection rates on exchange revenues, displayed liquidity, and retail execution quality. Identify the specific Rule 605 metrics the Commission will monitor post-rescission and the thresholds or patterns that would trigger regulatory intervention.
*Source: Ravnitzky*

### Structural Reform Recommendations

**4. Modernize Rule 605 Price Impact Measurement**
Permanently shorten the Rule 605 price impact measurement window from five minutes to one minute to reflect modern trading speeds and force accurate near-instantaneous adverse selection disclosure. This would empower retail brokers to identify and terminate relationships with underperforming wholesalers.
*Source: Irani (×3)*

**5. Mandate Pre-Internalization Venue Scanning**
Require wholesalers to systematically ping public exchanges and ATSs to confirm no superior passive midpoint liquidity is available before executing in a principal capacity. This addresses the empirically documented problem that 40-93% of internalized retail orders bypass superior available liquidity.
*Source: Irani (×3), Albert (×2)*

**6. Fully Implement Rule 612 Variable Sub-Penny Tick Sizes**
Restore competitive parity between public exchanges and off-exchange wholesalers by allowing exchanges to compete directly with wholesalers on sub-penny pricing. This addresses the structural competitive disadvantage that drove retail order flow off-exchange.
*Source: Irani (×3)*

**7. Develop a Technology-Neutral Framework for Tokenized NMS Equities**
Establish clear regulatory guidance on whether and how trade-through discipline applies to tokenized NMS equities trading on blockchain-based venues. The Commission should resolve the tension between Malinova & Park's argument (that blockchain incompatibility justifies rescission) and Martinez's argument (that the regulatory category should follow the underlying asset, not the token).
*Source: Malinova & Park, Martinez*

### Longer-Term Structural Considerations

**8. Address PFOF and Wholesaler Internalization Incentives**
The comment record strongly suggests that PFOF is the root cause of the displayed liquidity problem that Rule 611 has failed to solve. The Commission should consider whether phasing out or heavily restricting PFOF — consistent with the approach of G7 and OECD peer nations — would more effectively address the structural incentive failure than rescinding Rule 611.
*Source: Irani (×3), Martinez, Albert (×2)*

**9. Conduct AI-Specific Market Structure Analysis**
Before finalizing any reforms, conduct robust empirical analysis specifically addressing how AI and machine-learning systems in trading would behave in the absence of Rule 611's bright-line protections. Analyze the interaction between AI quoting strategies and displayed liquidity, and the potential for emergent behavior and feedback loops in an environment of locked and crossed quotations.
*Source: Kirchner (×2)*

**10. Consider Targeted Reforms Rather Than Wholesale Rescission**
Multiple commenters suggest that the Commission's diagnosis of the problem is correct but its remedy is disproportionate. Targeted reforms — such as a consolidated lit book with strict displayed-order priority, or a mandatory pre-internalization venue scanning requirement — may address the structural failures of the current framework without removing the only cross-venue price-discovery discipline in the equity markets.
*Source: Martinez, Kirchner, Ravnitzky*

## Conclusion

The comment record on the proposed rescission of Rule 611 and Rule 610(e) reveals a market structure debate of genuine complexity, obscured by the numerical dominance of brief retail investor opposition letters. When the record is analyzed by analytical depth and specificity rather than by headcount, a more nuanced picture emerges.

**The Commission's diagnosis commands broad agreement:** Rule 611 has failed over two decades to incentivize displayed liquidity. Off-exchange trading now exceeds 50% of NASDAQ volume. The maker-taker rebate structure is insufficient to compensate for the adverse-selection costs of posting visible quotes. The rule is structurally incompatible with emerging blockchain-based trading venues. These are not contested propositions in the substantive record.

**The Commission's remedy commands far less agreement.** The core objection — shared across academic economists, a specialized broker-dealer, a practicing retail financial adviser, and technically sophisticated individual commenters — is that outright rescission without a carefully designed replacement framework removes the only structural mechanism tying execution prices across venues to the best displayed quotation, while leaving the underlying incentive failure entirely unaddressed. The European experience provides empirical evidence that a weak best execution standard produces measurable, systematic execution quality shortfalls. The QCT exemption gap represents a specific, concrete harm to the complex options market that the Commission appears not to have considered.

**The retail investor record, while analytically thin, reflects a genuine and legitimate concern.** Retail investors do not control order routing, cannot audit execution algorithms, and cannot monitor execution quality across dozens of venues in real time. Rule 611 provides a hard, mechanical safeguard that does not depend on an investor's technological sophistication to be effective. Removing it without a proven replacement shifts the burden of protection onto the investors least equipped to bear it.

The Commission faces a genuine policy dilemma. The current framework is broken in ways that are well-documented and broadly acknowledged. But the proposed remedy risks accelerating the very trends it seeks to address — the flight to dark trading, the erosion of displayed liquidity, and the degradation of price discovery — unless it is accompanied by a robust replacement framework that addresses the structural incentive failures the current rule has failed to correct.

The most defensible path forward, based on the substantive comment record, is not outright rescission but a comprehensive restructuring: one that addresses the QCT exemption gap immediately, establishes a benchmark-anchored best execution obligation with meaningful enforcement teeth, mandates pre-internalization venue scanning, modernizes Rule 605 reporting, and develops a technology-neutral framework for tokenized NMS equities — all before or concurrent with any rescission of Rule 611. The Commission should also conduct the AI-specific empirical analysis that the current proposal lacks before dismantling protections that have underpinned U.S. equity markets for nearly two decades.

As Jeff Martinez writes: "The Commission is right that the present structure is broken. It is wrong that the answer is to remove the rule. The answer is to fix the incentive the rule failed to create."
